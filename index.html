ABOUT

Ellie Wyatt’s practice is primarily concerned with photography’s capacity for documenting the truth and for disseminating fictions. Drawing from resources including early photographic portraits, Google Image results of 1700s paintings, and censored photographs, she seeks to examine and amplify common themes that exist throughout eras and across imagery, such as the drape or the landscape as background.

Working from the earliest photographs of artworks through to contemporary media images of politicians, she examines the way photography is used to curate and create visual realities, considering the staged and the manipulated in its most benign and most sinister forms. She employs print as a medium that is inherently engaged in the conflict between the original and the reproduction, the analogue and the digital, and uses this to illustrate the paradox of digital decay as images become more widely circulated.

Using print to break down and amplify colours and shapes within individual photographs, the culmination of Wyatt’s research-led practice is often a curatorial exercise, where prints are presented alongside other more ephemeral media, such as photocopies. Borrowing display mechanisms from museums and information displays, Wyatt aims to create a relationship of inspection and examination between the work and the viewer.
